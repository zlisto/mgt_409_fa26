Homework 1: Small Business Valuation Agent

Scenario

Spoke & Wrench Bicycle Repair is a small New Haven shop. The owner keeps messy records—PDF receipts, bank and card statements, a lease, emails, and handwritten notes—not a clean QuickBooks export. Download the document pack (hw1_spoke_and_wrench.zip) and use AI to extract and organize what you can.

Your job is not just to build a pipeline. The documents disagree, overlap, and leave things out. You must reconcile conflicts, make defensible business-vs-personal calls, build January books, estimate value under multiple scenarios, and explain where your agent was wrong or unsure. AI-maxxing is encouraged; grades reward judgment and verification, not a one-shot zip of JSON files.

Download data: hw1_spoke_and_wrench.zip (unzip before running your agent).

Submit: one zip file, hw1.zip — see Problem 10.

Problem 1 — Set up your project (10 points)

Turn in a Python project we can run from the terminal: agent.py (reads documents and writes data files), report.py (builds the valuation webpage from those files), requirements.txt, README.md, and a prompts/ folder with the plain-text instructions you give your AI when extracting from PDFs and emails.

We will run: python agent.py --docs-dir PATH --out-dir output then python report.py --json-dir output --out output/valuation.html. Both must complete without errors. Do not put API keys in your zip; use environment variables and document them in your README.

Problem 2 — Receipt ledger (12 points)

Build output/receipts.json from the purchase receipt PDFs in the document pack: Park Tool, New Haven Bike Parts, Home Depot, Speedy Courier, Amazon headphones, and the eBay repair-stand confirmation. These are proof-of-purchase documents—not the bank statement, credit card statement, lease, IOU, or Hartford Mutual quote.

For each line item, record vendor, date, description, amount_usd, category (your best tag, e.g. tools_equipment, shipping, personal_electronics), and source_file. If a field is missing from the PDF, list it under fields_not_found—do not invent values.

The receipt ledger is a catalog of what each document says. Several amounts also appear on the bank or card statements; note that in your data if helpful, but do not decide here whether something is business vs personal or whether it hits January profit and loss—that is Problems 3 and 4.

Problem 3 — Bank and credit card classification (12 points)

Read the January bank statement and credit card statement in the document pack. Turn each posted line into structured data you can use later for reconciliation and the profit and loss statement (P&L)—also called an income statement: revenue minus expenses for the month.

Bank transactions. Extract every line from bank_statement_jan2026.pdf. For each deposit or withdrawal, record the date, description, amount, whether it is business or personal, and how you would book it (revenue, expense, owner draw, and so on). Save the result as output/bank_transactions.json.

Credit card transactions. Extract every charge from credit_card_jan2026.pdf. For each line, record the date, merchant, amount, whether it is business or personal, and—if business—what type of shop expense it is. Save the result as output/credit_card_transactions.json.

Both files — every row:

  • classification: business or personal

Bank rows — also include:

  • direction: credit (deposit) or debit (withdrawal)
  • accounting_label — for credits: revenue, owner_contribution, transfer, or other; for debits: expense, owner_draw, transfer, or other
  • expense_type — when accounting_label is expense, a short label (e.g. rent, utilities, cogs_parts)

Credit card rows — also include:

  • expense_category — for rows you classified business (e.g. shipping, tools_equipment); omit or set null for personal rows

Issuer categories on the card statement (e.g. “Grocery stores,” “Gas stations”) are automated MCC labels—they do not tell you business vs personal. You must cross-check statements against other documents in the pack, including:

  • Your receipt ledger (receipts.json from Problem 2) — match amounts and items to card or bank lines (e.g. Amazon headphones ↔ 01/04 card charge). Re-read a receipt PDF only if something is missing from the ledger.
  • Emails — especially email_owner_voice_memo.txt, where Jordan explains mixed Square deposits, REI apparel, Home Depot supplies, and personal Amazon purchases

Problem 4 — Reconciliation log (18 points)

Reconcile your receipt ledger (Problem 2) with your bank and credit card transactions (Problem 3). Cross-check emails and other documents in the pack when amounts or categories do not line up. Save the result as output/reconciliation_log.json.

List every amount that appears in more than one place. Some rows will have unclear categories or different dollar amounts across sources—record what each source shows, whether it belongs in January profit and loss (P&L), and the single amount you will use. For each item include:

  • id — short label you choose
  • sources — file names where you saw it
  • amounts_seen — what each source shows
  • included_in_january_p_and_l — yes if this item is a business revenue or expense for January 2026 that belongs on your income statement (Problem 6); no if you exclude it (e.g. personal spending, future pipeline work, or off-books items not in January books)
  • amount_used_in_p_and_l — the single dollar amount you count in January P&L when included_in_january_p_and_l is yes; use 0 when no
  • resolution — plain English: what you did and why

Problem 5 — Judgment calls (10 points)

Some reconciliation rows need a judgment call—the documents do not settle on their own whether an amount belongs in January P&L or how much to count. After reconciling (Problem 4), pick three rows where you found it hardest to set included_in_january_p_and_l or amount_used_in_p_and_l, and explain why. Save the result as output/judgment_calls.json.

For each of the three judgment calls, include:

  • transaction_id — use the id from that row in your reconciliation log
  • included_in_january_p_and_l — your decision: yes or no
  • amount_used_in_p_and_l — the dollar amount you count in January P&L, or 0 if excluded
  • evidence_for — bullet list of facts and document names that support your decision
  • confidence — how sure you are: high, medium, or low (at least one of your three calls must be low)

Problem 6 — January income statement (10 points)

Build the shop’s January 2026 profit and loss (P&L)—also called an income statement: total business revenue, expense lines, and net income. Save the result as output/income_statement_jan2026.json.

Build your income statement from reconciliation_log.json (Problem 4). For each row where included_in_january_p_and_l is yes, use amount_used_in_p_and_l and roll it into revenue or expense lines.

Your JSON must include:

  • period — 2026-01
  • revenue_usd — total January business revenue
  • expense_lines — array of expense rows; each row needs:
    • label — short name for the line (e.g. rent, card_tools_equipment)
    • amount_usd — dollars for this line
    • category — expense type (e.g. rent_occupancy, tools_equipment, cogs_parts, shipping)
    • sources — file names backing this line (e.g. bank_statement_jan2026.pdf, credit_card_jan2026.pdf)
  • total_expenses_usd — sum of all expense lines
  • net_income_usd — revenue_usd minus total_expenses_usd

Problem 7 — Valuation scenarios (12 points)

You have rebuilt January books. The next question is different: what is this business worth? There is no single correct number. Value depends on who is asking and what they believe about future cash the shop can generate.

For a small shop like this, a common approach is annualized profit × a multiplier. Take January net income from Problem 6, multiply by 12 for a rough full-year earnings figure, then apply a multiple (e.g. 2×–3.5×) to get a purchase price range. In each scenario, low_usd and high_usd are what someone might pay to buy the whole business—not monthly or annual profit. Explain your multiple and any add-ons in method.

Different readers trust the numbers differently. A buyer weighs earnings quality, future jobs not yet collected, and downside risk. A bank focuses on reliable cash flow, collateral, and personal spending mixed with the business. An owner may count reputation and booked work that is not in January revenue. Your three scenarios— books_only, adjusted, and skeptical_buyer—should reflect those different levels of trust.

Save the result as output/valuation_inputs.json. Required sections:

  • booked_january_net_income_usd — net_income_usd from your January income statement (Problem 6)
  • annualized_net_income_usd — that figure × 12; the earnings base for your multiplier math
  • off_books_receivables — money customers already owe for work completed but not yet deposited (e.g. an IOU or unpaid invoice)
  • pipeline_orders — future jobs from customer emails (not January revenue); note when sources disagree on price or timing
  • excluded_from_valuation — intangibles, missing documents, or risks a buyer or lender would still ask about
  • valuation_scenarios — three scenario objects. Each includes:
    • low_usd — the low end of what someone might pay to buy the shop
    • high_usd — the high end of that purchase-price range
    • method — plain English showing your math: annualized net income, multiple, and any receivables, pipeline, or discounts you added
    Name the three scenarios:
    • books_only — annualized net income × multiple only
    • adjusted — add receivables and probability-weighted pipeline
    • skeptical_buyer — lower multiple, heavier discounts
  • assumptions — pipeline probabilities and other shared judgments (list once here); each scenario’s method shows how you applied them to get that scenario’s range

For adjusted, assign each pipeline order a probability between 0 and 1: your estimate that the job will happen and pay roughly the emailed amount (e.g. 0.75 probability on a $4,000 job would be counted as $3,000). Show the math in each scenario’s method. A single unexplained range is not enough.

Problem 8 — Valuation webpage (5 points)

Your JSON files hold the analysis; a buyer or banker will not read raw JSON. Turn your work into a one-page valuation briefing they can open in any browser—a readable summary of what you found and what you think the shop is worth.

report.py should read your output/ JSON files and build that page. Save the result as output/valuation.html. Use the same numbers as your JSON—do not hand-edit totals in the HTML.

The page should include:

  • January P&L — revenue, expenses, and net income from your income statement
  • Personal vs business — bank and card rows you classified as personal (what you excluded from the shop books)
  • Pipeline orders — future jobs from Problem 7
  • Valuation scenarios — your three purchase-price ranges (books_only, adjusted, skeptical_buyer) with methods
  • Missing documents and risks — items from excluded_from_valuation

Problem 9 — Memos (8 points)

Your JSON and webpage summarize the numbers; a real deal still needs a written recommendation. Write two short memos (about 250–400 words each) for different readers who would use your analysis differently.

Buyer memo. Save as output/memo_buyer.md. You are advising a potential acquirer: How strong is January earnings? What pipeline orders add upside? What is the top risk if they buy the shop?

Bank memo. Save as output/memo_bank.md. You are advising a lender considering a loan to the business: Is personal spending mixed with the shop? How large are owner draws? What collateral or financial detail is not in these PDFs?
