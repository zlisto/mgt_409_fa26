Homework 1: Small Business Valuation Agent

Scenario

Spoke & Wrench Bicycle Repair is a small New Haven business whose records arrive in many formats—not as a clean export from accounting software. Canvas provides hw1_spoke_and_wrench.zip, containing PDFs of various business documents and a set of business emails.

In this assignment you will use AI to read those documents, organize the financial information, estimate what the business may be worth, and present your work as data files, a simple webpage, and a short memo. You will submit everything in one zip file as described at the bottom of this page.

Submit: one zip file, hw1.zip — see Problem 8.

Problem 1 — Set up your project (10 points)

Turn in a Python project we can run from the terminal. It must include two files—agent.py (reads the Canvas documents and writes your data files) and report.py (builds the valuation webpage from those data files). Also include requirements.txt (lists any Python packages needed), README.md (step-by-step instructions for installing and running your code), and a folder called prompts containing plain-text files with the instructions you give to your AI agent when extracting data from PDFs and emails.

We will unzip your submission, install dependencies, and run two commands. First, python agent.py --docs-dir PATH --out-dir sample_output, where PATH is the unzipped Canvas folder. That should create the data files in a folder called sample_output. Second, python report.py --json-dir sample_output --out sample_output/valuation.html, which should create the webpage from those data files without opening the PDFs again. Both steps should complete without errors. Do not put API keys in your zip; use environment variables and explain them in your README for other users who want to utilize your agent.

Problem 2 — Receipt ledger (20 points)

Read the receipt PDFs and the eBay confirmation, and save a tidy list of purchases in sample_output/receipts.json. For each receipt, record who you paid (vendor), the date, a short description, the dollar amount, what type of expense it was (category—for example parts, tools, or supplies), and which PDF it came from (source_file). See the format example below. If something is missing from the PDF, say so in fields_not_found instead of filling in a guess.

Problem 3 — Bank and credit card classification (20 points)

Read the bank statement PDF and the credit card statement PDF. Save two data files in the folder sample_output: bank_transactions.json and credit_card_transactions.json. For every line on each statement, label it business or personal. For bank deposits, also say whether the money looks like shop revenue or an owner withdrawal. For business charges on the credit card, add a short label for the type of expense (for example, parts or shipping). The owner mixes personal and shop spending on one card, so thoughtfulness here matters for your grade.

Problem 4 — January income statement (15 points)

Using your work from Problem 3 (and the lease PDF), build a simple January 2026 profit-and-loss summary in sample_output/income_statement_jan2026.json. Report total revenue, a list of expense lines, and net income for the month. The math must add up. In an assumptions section, explain any judgment calls—for example, how you treated money the owner took out of the business, or whether you counted the same purchase twice on a receipt and a statement.

Problem 5 — Valuation inputs (10 points)

Save sample_output/valuation_inputs.json combining your January results with the business emails and the IOU PDF. List future jobs mentioned in the emails as pipeline_orders—these are not January revenue. List money owed to the shop but not on the bank statement (such as the IOU) under off_books_receivables. Note what a buyer could not learn from these PDFs alone—things like the owner’s reputation—in excluded_from_valuation. End with a low and high estimate of business value and a short explanation of how you got there.

Problem 6 — Valuation webpage (10 points)

Create sample_output/valuation.html, a webpage you can open in any browser (no hosting required). It should summarize your January profit and loss, show how you split personal vs business spending, list future orders from the emails, state your valuation range, and note what documents are still missing. Use the same numbers as your data files; do not add new figures on the webpage.

Problem 7 — Investment memo (10 points)

Write memo.md (about 400–600 words) as if you were advising someone thinking of buying the shop. Describe the one transaction you found hardest to call business vs personal, and why. Explain what the emails and IOU add to your valuation that January statements alone do not show. Name one number your AI might get wrong and how you checked it. Finally, name one document you would still want before signing a deal, and why.

Problem 8 — Submission format (5 points)

Create a folder on your computer named hw1. Put all of your homework files inside that folder using the layout below—same folder names, same file names. When everything is in place, zip the entire hw1 folder (not the individual files on their own) and upload the resulting hw1.zip to Canvas. When we unzip your submission, we should see one top-level folder called hw1 containing your code, prompts, memo, and completed sample_output files.
